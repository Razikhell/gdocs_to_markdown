**How Spotify Wrapped interacts with your data.**

Spotify, Apple Music, and many other programs use AI algorithms to create personalized “wraps” for every individual listener. A “wrapped” is a summary of information that you spit out every time you listen to music.  These summaries include information collected from January 1st to October 31st.  Typically, the data it collects includes metrics on what songs you play, artists of said songs, genres listened to, total minutes listened, average listening time per day, and many more. Today, I’ll be talking about how the way Spotify Wrapped tracks your information can have a negative effect on your psych. 

Personally I think Spotify Wrapped is a fun way to track listening, but I think it also has some negative effects on the listener. It makes listening more of a competition and can completely rid music of its emotional value. The competitiveness of it all also causes users to inflate their favorite artists’ statistics, creating a sense of popularity that doesn’t exist. 

I’d say Spotify largely affects both your social media presence and sometimes it can even seep into your personal life. On many social media platforms, people have begun creating personalities for music. For example if you listen to artists like Deftones or Metallica, people will call you a poser or assume you’re an annoying person because they’ve met others who listen to those artists and fit those personality traits. This creates a negative environment where listeners try to listen to music considered average  rather than what they actually like so they don’t feel judged. 

Another issue with Spotify Wrapped is that it likes to fit you in boxes, in categories. It’ll tell you what personality type you have based on your music, but it doesn’t actually  know you. The ai can’t tell whether you listened to a song because you were sad, or if you just fell asleep. It has no concept of emotional importance so it can’t accurately judge you on what it intends to. Psychologically, this can have a negative effect on you. You’ll begin to unconsciously try and fit that personality because you think that’s how you naturally act. 

My last point is that overall, everything Spotify Wrapped tries to tell you about yourself is false. At the end of the day, it’s just trying to give you a surge of dopamine by creating semi-fake stats so that you continue to use the app the next year. It can’t judge your listening when it doesn’t know how you’ve changed in the days between October 31st and January 1st. All of its efforts are just cleverly disguised marketing to get people talking so more people join the app. 

I admit, there are some good things about the program. It can help you connect with others through similar music taste, creating a sense of community or belonging. It can reconnect you to songs that may have memories or emotions tied to them that maybe you forgot about, giving you a feeling of nostalgia. It can even help you discover songs in different languages you didn’t know existed previously. In the end, I do like Spotify Wrapped. After all, it’s up to you what music apps you use or even if you prefer CDs. This opinion piece is more so on how Spotify Wrapped can be negative, not that it definitively is for everyone.

In conclusion, I’d say that if you are going to use a music app every single day for a long time, you should know how it could negatively or positively affect you.
