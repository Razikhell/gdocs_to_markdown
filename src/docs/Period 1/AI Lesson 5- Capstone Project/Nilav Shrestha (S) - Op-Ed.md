\# TikTok's AI Algorithm: Why We Need Rules Before It's Too Late

\*\*Hook:\*\*  
Imagine a friend who watches your every move—every smile, every frown, every time you look away—then uses that information to control what you see for hours each day. You'd call that friend manipulative. Yet millions of us let TikTok's artificial intelligence do exactly this every single day.

\*\*Clear Argument:\*\*  
TikTok's recommendation AI, which powers the app's addictive "For You" page, is one of the most powerful behavior-control systems ever created. While it seems harmless and fun, this technology is damaging mental health, dividing our society, and hurting how young people grow up. We need new laws to control it now.

\*\*How the Algorithm Works:\*\*  
TikTok's AI works like a mind reader that gets smarter every second. Think of it as a super-fast student studying you for a test. It tracks everything: how long you watch each video (even if it's just two seconds), what you rewatch, what you like or skip, and what hashtags you click on. It feeds all this information into a machine learning system that predicts what will keep you scrolling. Unlike Instagram or Facebook, which mostly show posts from people you follow, TikTok's AI chooses almost everything you see based on your behavior—even from strangers—simply because it thinks you'll watch it.

\*\*Point 1: Hurting Young People's Mental Health\*\*  
First, TikTok's algorithm will cause a dramatic increase in mental health problems among young people over the next five years. The AI is designed to maximize "watch time," so it learns to show content that creates strong emotions—often negative ones. A 2023 study from the University of Minnesota found that TikTok's algorithm pushes content about depression, anxiety, and eating disorders to users who show even slight interest in these topics. When a teenager watches one video about feeling sad or hating their body, the algorithm floods their feed with more of the same. This creates a dangerous cycle. 

\*\*Prediction:\*\* By 2030, we will see teen depression and anxiety rates increase by another 30-40%, with TikTok usage being identified as a major contributing factor. Teen depression has already increased by 25% since 2019 according to the CDC, and 67% of American teenagers now use TikTok. As the algorithm gets better at finding and exploiting what makes kids feel bad, mental health professionals predict we'll see record numbers of young people requiring therapy and medication. Eating disorder treatment centers are already reporting that 80% of their teen patients cite social media, particularly TikTok, as a trigger. Without intervention, we're heading toward a generation where mental health crises become the norm rather than the exception.

\*\*Recommendation for Point 1:\*\* Congress should require TikTok and similar platforms to implement "wellbeing-first algorithms" for all users under 18\. Specifically, the algorithm must be redesigned to limit exposure to harmful content categories. If a user watches more than three videos about depression, eating disorders, or self-harm within an hour, the algorithm must stop recommending similar content and instead show diverse, positive material. The platform should also be required to insert mental health resources after every two videos on sensitive topics. Independent auditors should verify compliance monthly, with fines of $10,000 per violation. This directly addresses the feedback loop that currently traps vulnerable teens in cycles of harmful content.

\*\*Point 2: Dividing America and Spreading Lies\*\*  
Second, TikTok's AI will make political division in America significantly worse, potentially threatening our ability to function as a democracy. The algorithm creates extreme "filter bubbles"—personalized worlds where everyone sees completely different information. A 2024 MIT study found that TikTok users searching for the same topic got totally different results based on their watch history. Some users only saw videos supporting one political view, while others only saw the opposite. This happens because the algorithm notices when you scroll past content you disagree with, so it stops showing you anything that challenges your beliefs. 

\*\*Prediction:\*\* By the 2028 presidential election, TikTok's algorithm will have created such separate information realities that Americans will be unable to agree on basic facts about major issues. Already, 40% of Gen Z gets their news from TikTok according to Pew Research. Political scientists predict that as this percentage grows to 60-70% by 2028, we'll see unprecedented levels of political polarization. Voters will not just disagree on solutions—they'll disagree on what problems exist at all. We can expect increased political violence, as people who live in completely different factual universes see each other not as fellow citizens with different views, but as threats to reality itself. Foreign actors will exploit this by creating divisive content that the algorithm happily amplifies because it generates engagement.

\*\*Recommendation for Point 2:\*\* Congress should mandate "viewpoint diversity requirements" for content recommendation algorithms. For every ten videos on political or news topics, the algorithm must include at least two videos presenting different perspectives from what the user typically engages with. Additionally, platforms must clearly label content as "opinion" versus "news" and show users a monthly "filter bubble report" that reveals what perspectives they've been exposed to versus what they've missed. TikTok should also be required to offer a "balanced news mode" that prioritizes factual accuracy over engagement when users are viewing news content. This regulation would force the algorithm to expose users to diverse viewpoints, helping maintain the shared reality democracy requires.

\*\*Point 3: Preventing Kids from Becoming Themselves\*\*  
Third, TikTok's algorithm will fundamentally change how the next generation develops their identities, creating adults who struggle with independent thinking and decision-making. Psychologists say that teenage years are when people figure out their own interests, values, and beliefs through exploration. But TikTok's AI skips this process by constantly predicting what you want before you decide for yourself. It's like having someone else pick every book you read based on tracking your eye movements, never letting you browse the library yourself. A 2023 study in \*Developmental Psychology\* found that heavy TikTok users aged 13-16 had more trouble focusing on their own and felt less confident about their own preferences compared to teens who didn't use the app as much. 

\*\*Prediction:\*\* Children who grow up with TikTok's algorithm during their formative years (roughly 2020-2030) will show measurably reduced capacity for self-directed learning and autonomous decision-making as adults. Since teenage brains are still developing—especially the part that handles decision-making—they're extra vulnerable to this kind of influence. By 2035, we'll see this generation entering the workforce and struggling with tasks that require independent judgment, creativity, and the ability to focus without constant algorithmic guidance. Employers will report difficulties with workers who need constant external validation and direction. Universities are already noticing that students who grew up on TikTok have shorter attention spans and more difficulty choosing their own research topics. We're essentially outsourcing identity formation to an AI designed not to help people flourish, but to keep them watching—and the long-term consequences will reshape what it means to be an autonomous adult.

\*\*Recommendation for Point 3:\*\* Congress should require platforms to provide mandatory "exploration modes" for users under 18, where the algorithm is significantly weakened. For at least 30% of their time on the app, young users should see content selected randomly or chronologically rather than by AI prediction. This gives teens the chance to discover interests organically rather than having everything pre-selected. Additionally, platforms should implement "decision-making breaks" every 20 minutes that ask users to actively choose what type of content they want to see next, rather than passively consuming what the algorithm serves. Schools should also receive federal funding to teach "algorithmic literacy" classes that help students understand how recommendation systems work and develop strategies for maintaining autonomy. These measures would preserve the developmental space young people need to form their own identities.

\*\*To Be Sure:\*\*  
To be sure, TikTok's algorithm isn't all bad. The platform has helped regular people become successful creators without needing connections or money. It's supported important movements like \#BlackLivesMatter, helped small businesses find customers, and brought entertainment to millions during COVID lockdowns. The algorithm has helped people find communities around unique interests. Some teachers even use TikTok to make learning fun. The technology itself isn't evil—it could be designed to help people instead of just keeping them hooked. The question is how we choose to control it.

\*\*Call to Action:\*\*  
We need immediate action to implement these regulations before TikTok's algorithm causes irreversible harm to an entire generation. Here's how you can help:

\*\*What Congress Must Do:\*\*  
Pass the "Algorithmic Accountability and Youth Protection Act" that includes:  
1\. \*\*Wellbeing-first algorithms for minors\*\* that limit harmful content loops and prioritize mental health  
2\. \*\*Viewpoint diversity requirements\*\* that burst filter bubbles and expose users to different perspectives  
3\. \*\*Mandatory exploration modes\*\* that give young people space to develop independent preferences  
4\. \*\*Full algorithmic transparency\*\* allowing independent researchers to study effects on users  
5\. \*\*Significant penalties\*\* ($100,000 per day of non-compliance) to ensure platforms take these rules seriously

\*\*What You Can Do Right Now:\*\*

\*\*Contact Your Representatives:\*\* Call or email your congressional representatives today. Search "contact my representative" online to find their information. Tell them: "I'm concerned about TikTok's algorithm harming young people's mental health and development. Please support legislation requiring algorithmic accountability and youth protection measures." Personal calls and emails from constituents are one of the most effective ways to influence lawmakers.

\*\*Support Advocacy Organizations:\*\* Donate to or volunteer with organizations fighting for these changes:  
\- \*\*Center for Humane Technology\*\* (humanetech.com) \- Leading the movement for ethical technology design  
\- \*\*Common Sense Media\*\* (commonsensemedia.org) \- Advocating for child safety online  
\- \*\*Fairplay\*\* (fairplayforkids.org) \- Fighting manipulative marketing to children  
Even $10 donations help these organizations lobby Congress and run public awareness campaigns.

\*\*Educate Your Community:\*\* Talk to parents, teachers, and young people about how the algorithm works. Host a community meeting at your local library or school. Share this article. Knowledge is power—when people understand they're being manipulated, they can resist it. Ask your school board to implement algorithmic literacy education.

\*\*Model Better Behavior:\*\* Set limits on your own TikTok use. Use screen time controls on your phone to limit the app to 30 minutes daily. Choose chronological feeds when available. Show young people in your life that it's possible to use technology intentionally rather than compulsively.

\*\*Pressure TikTok Directly:\*\* Leave reviews on app stores demanding these features. Tweet at @TikTok asking for wellbeing-first algorithms and exploration modes. Companies respond to public pressure—make noise.

\*\*Vote:\*\* In upcoming elections, ask candidates about their positions on regulating social media algorithms. Make this a voting issue. Politicians respond to what voters care about.

TikTok's algorithm shows how powerful AI has become at controlling human behavior. We can either create rules now to make this technology serve people's wellbeing, or wait until an entire generation has been raised by an AI that sees them as attention to extract, not humans to help. 

The predictions are clear: without action, we face a future of widespread mental health crises, democratic collapse, and a generation unable to think for themselves. But the solutions are also clear, and they're achievable if we act now.

The choice is ours, and we need to make it today. Our children's futures depend on it.
