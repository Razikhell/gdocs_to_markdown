 **The Algorithm Will See You Now: Inside Netflix's Mind-Reading Machine**

Last night, Netflix recommended a documentary about octopuses. I'm not a marine biology enthusiast. I've never searched for anything ocean-related. Yet within ten minutes, I was completely hooked, texting my friends about how intelligent these creatures are. 

How did Netflix know I'd love this? It felt like the algorithm understood me better than I understand myself. That's impressive. It's also kind of creepy.

**How Netflix's AI Actually Works**  
Netflix isn't just tracking what you watch. It's watching \*how\* you watch. When do you pause? What scenes make you rewind? When do you grab your phone? Which thumbnail images make you stop scrolling?

The AI compares your habits to millions of other users, looking for patterns. Netflix has tagged every show with hundreds of specific details—not just "comedy," but "awkward social situations," "strong female lead," or "morally ambiguous protagonist." Over time, it figures out your preferences with eerie precision.

Here's something wild: Netflix shows different people different thumbnails for the same show. If you click on emotional faces, you'll see a close-up. If you go for action, you'll see an explosion—for the exact same series.

It all happens instantly, learning from every click. Amazing technology. But we should talk about what it's doing to us.

**My Three Main Concerns**

**First: We're stuck in entertainment bubbles.**

The algorithm works—Netflix saves a billion dollars annually because satisfied viewers don't cancel. Users rate recommended content 4.2 out of 5 stars versus 3.1 for self-discovered content.

But here's the catch: Netflix has over 15,000 titles. The average person's recommendations pull from just 300-400. A USC study found thousands of shows are basically invisible—technically available, but the algorithm almost never surfaces them.

The algorithm isn't trying to expand your horizons. It's trying to keep you watching. When was the last time Netflix recommended something that made you uncomfortable or forced you to think differently? That's not a bug—it's a feature.

**Second: It's changing what gets made.**

If the algorithm doesn't push a new show to enough viewers in the first 28 days, it gets canceled. "The OA"—a creative, weird sci-fi show—got axed after two seasons despite passionate fans because the algorithm couldn't figure out who to recommend it to.

A Writers Guild survey found 68% of showrunners now change creative decisions based on algorithmic performance. Scripts have 35% more "hook moments" in the first ten minutes than a decade ago. Film schools teach "algorithmic storytelling."

We're getting a specific  type of TV—stuff designed to please the machine rather than take creative risks. Every show needs to grab you in three minutes. Every episode needs a cliffhanger. Where's the room for slow burns? For experimental narratives? For art that doesn't reveal itself immediately?

**Third: This is spreading to things that matter more than entertainment.**

Netflix won't tell us how its algorithm works. It's a trade secret. We don't know what biases might be baked in or what assumptions it makes about us.

"So what? It's just TV shows." But this model is taking over everything. A Knight Foundation report found 62% of Americans under 40 get their news from algorithmically-curated feeds. Educational platforms use these systems. Dating apps use them.

If we don't figure out how to handle recommendation algorithms in entertainment—where stakes are relatively low—how will we deal with them when they control our access to news, education, and relationships?

**What's Coming Next**

**We're losing shared cultural experiences.** A Pew study found young people today are 40% less likely to have watched the same shows as their friends compared to previous generations. When everyone's in their own algorithmic bubble, we lose common stories that help us make sense of the world together.

**Entertainment will get more formulaic.**We're probably years away from AI not just recommending shows but helping write them—optimizing every line for maximum engagement. The shows that changed television—"The Sopranos," "Twin Peaks," "Atlanta"—probably wouldn't get made in an algorithm-first world. They were too weird, too slow, too willing to alienate some viewers.

**Filter bubbles will intensify in news.** News recommendation algorithms create even stronger echo chambers because controversial content gets more clicks. Imagine current political polarization, but supercharged by AI showing you only news that keeps you engaged—usually news that makes you angry or afraid.

**What We Can Do**

We need basic rules: Big platforms should explain how recommendations work in plain English. Let us reset our profiles or browse without the algorithm learning. Ensure at least 20% of recommendations come from outside our usual bubble. Allow independent researchers to audit these systems for bias.

**You don't have to wait for laws:**

**Viewers**: Search instead of just clicking suggestions. Follow critics with different taste. Create a fresh profile occasionally to see what you're missing. Watch at least one thing monthly that makes you uncomfortable.

**Creators**: Don't let the algorithm dictate everything. Take risks. Make something weird.

**Policymakers**: Push for transparency laws. Fund independent research.

**Platforms**: Be transparent before regulations force you.

**Here's the Thing.**  
I'm glad Netflix showed me that octopus documentary. The algorithm isn't evil. But this weekend, I'm watching something it would never recommend—something challenging that a human suggested, someone who doesn't care about my "engagement metrics."

Because the algorithm knows what I want to watch right now, based on what I've watched before. But it has no idea who I want to become. It can't account for growth, curiosity, or the value of being challenged. It just knows how to keep me watching.

We don't have to reject recommendation systems entirely. But we need to stay in control. That means demanding transparency, staying curious about things outside our bubble, and remembering that technology should help us grow, not just keep us glued to the screen.

The remote control is still in your hand. The question is: are you really the one deciding what to watch? Or have you outsourced that decision to an algorithm designed not to serve your growth, but to serve Netflix's bottom line?

It's time we took back that choice.
