**Title Ai’s impact on people**

Op/Ed

When Apple introduced Siri in 2011, it felt like science fiction had arrived in our pockets. Here was a digital assistant that could understand natural language, crack jokes, and actually help you get things done—all with a tap of a button. Thirteen years later, Siri has become something else entirely: a cautionary tale about squandered potential.

Don't get me wrong—Siri still handles the basics. Set a timer? Sure. Play a song? Most of the time. But in an era when ChatGPT can write poetry, debug code, and engage in nuanced conversation, Siri still struggles to understand follow-up questions or execute simple multi-step commands without getting confused.

The problem isn't that Siri has gotten worse. It's that Siri has barely gotten better while the world around it has transformed.

Apple's competitors haven't been standing still. Google Assistant leverages the company's search dominance and AI research to provide genuinely helpful contextual information. Amazon's Alexa has built an entire smart home ecosystem. And now, AI chatbots have raised the bar for what we expect from conversational interfaces.

Meanwhile, Siri remains frustratingly literal-minded. Ask it to "remind me about this when I get home," while looking at a website, and you're likely to get a reminder that says "this" rather than capturing the actual content you wanted to remember. Request something slightly outside its narrow parameters, and you'll get the dreaded "I can't help with that" or a web search you didn't ask for.

Apple defenders often point to privacy as the reason for Siri's limitations. The company's commitment to on-device processing and minimal data collection is admirable—genuinely so. In an age of surveillance capitalism, Apple's privacy stance is a competitive advantage and a moral imperative.

But privacy and capability aren't mutually exclusive. Apple has the resources, talent, and technology to build a more capable assistant while maintaining its privacy principles. The company has made remarkable advances in on-device machine learning. Its custom silicon is industry-leading. The excuse that privacy requires mediocrity simply doesn't hold water.  
What's most frustrating about Siri's stagnation is what it represents: Apple's willingness to let a once-revolutionary product coast on brand loyalty. The company that taught us to "think different" seems content to let Siri remain stubbornly the same.

This matters because voice assistants aren't a novelty anymore—they're infrastructure. They're how we interact with our homes, cars, and devices. They're becoming the primary interface for people with accessibility needs. A mediocre assistant isn't just disappointing; it's a barrier.

Apple has reportedly been working on AI improvements, with rumors of a Siri overhaul in the works. But we've heard this before. Year after year, WWDC presentations promise Siri enhancements that amount to minor feature additions rather than fundamental improvements.

Here's what Apple needs to do: Stop treating Siri as a feature and start treating it as a platform. Open it up to deeper third-party integration. Invest in the kind of large language model technology that has made recent AI breakthroughs possible. And most importantly, set a new standard for what a privacy-respecting AI assistant can be.

The technology exists. The need exists. What's missing is urgency.

Siri was once a glimpse of the future. Now it's a reminder of how quickly the future can pass you by if you're not paying attention. Apple revolutionized the smartphone, the tablet, and the smartwatch. It's time to revolutionize Siri—or risk watching it become irrelevant.

The voice of the future shouldn't sound like it's stuck in the past.
