**How ChatGPT is Impacting Our Future**

Op/Ed

Every day, millions of students, workers, and creators open ChatGPT and ask it to write something for them. Maybe it's an email to a boss, an essay for English class, or even a birthday message to a friend. Since OpenAI launched ChatGPT in November 2022, it has become the fastest-growing app in history, reaching 100 million users in just two months. But have you ever stopped to think about how this chatbot might be changing our entire society? I would argue that ChatGPT is quietly revolutionizing how we think, work, and communicate. As users of this technology, we need to understand these changes and take steps to protect our skills and our future.

Think of ChatGPT like an incredibly well-read student who has memorized billions of books, articles, and conversations, but doesn't actually understand what any of it means. When you ask a question, it's not thinking or reasoning like a human—instead, it's playing an extremely sophisticated game of "what word comes next?" based on all the patterns it has seen before. Imagine if you read every book ever written and then someone asked you to finish the sentence "The capital of France is..."—you'd probably say "Paris" not because you understand geography, but because you've seen those words together thousands of times. ChatGPT works the same way, predicting the most likely next word, then the next, then the next, until it has constructed a complete response that sounds intelligent and human-like. It's important to remember that it's all based on probability and patterns, not genuine understanding or thinking. This is why ChatGPT can sometimes sound confident while being completely wrong—it's just predicting what words should come next based on patterns, not actually knowing whether those words are true.

I believe that within the next ten years, society could split into two groups: people who use AI as a tool to enhance their abilities, and people who've become so dependent on AI that they can't function without it. Think about it like using a calculator for every single math problem, you’d never learn to do math in your head. Students who use ChatGPT to write all their essays never learn how to organize their thoughts or develop arguments. Teachers are already reporting students who get straight A's on written assignments but can't speak coherently about the topics in class. Entry-level jobs where young people used to learn workplace skills are disappearing because companies don't need junior copywriters or paralegals when AI can do that work. By 2036, we could have a generation with impressive resumes but no actual skills, competing desperately for the few jobs that still require human workers.

I believe that ChatGPT could reach a point where it is nearly impossible to know what's made by an AI and what isn’t. Right now, you can usually tell when something online is fake because it has errors, sounds weird, or doesn’t look right. ChatGPT can already write fake news articles that sound exactly like real journalism to most people and generate fake research papers with made-up statistics that seem totally believable. The problem is that AI lies with perfect confidence, stating complete falsehoods with the same calm authority it uses for facts. Political campaigns are already using AI to generate thousands of unique, targeted messages for different voters. When you can't trust anything you read, how do you make any informed decisions?

ChatGPT could make real human connection rare. We're heading toward a world where most daily communication isn't actually with humans—customer service chats, emails from teachers, and even messages from friends might be AI-generated. Some people already use ChatGPT to write their dating app messages, breakup texts, and apologies. Real human connection requires vulnerability and imperfection—things AI can fake but never truly provide. Studies show that loneliness among young people has reached record levels, even though we're more "connected" than ever. In twenty years, genuine human interaction might become much rarer than we think.

OpenAI and other AI developers could stop trying to make ChatGPT indistinguishable from humans. Other AI developers could build in clear markers that identify AI-generated content, like watermarks that can't be removed. They could add "friction points" before ChatGPT writes essays or generates medical advice, asking users to confirm they understand the risks. AI companies could invest as much in detection tools as they do in generation tools. If OpenAI can build technology that writes like a human, I believe they have an obligation to help people identify when it's been used. Doing these things could greatly reduce or even prevent the damage done in the future.

We users should develop "AI wisdom"—knowing not just how to use ChatGPT, but when we should and shouldn't use it. You could use ChatGPT to brainstorm ideas, but you should also  do your own thinking and writing for the final product. Let it help with routine tasks, but preserve challenging work that builds your skills. Us users should always be honest about when you've used AI, especially in school or work situations. Practice your human skills intentionally by writing without AI assistance and having face-to-face conversations. Think of it like exercise—you have to work your mental muscles, or they'll atrophy.

Despite what this article makes it look like, ChatGPT isn't all bad—it provides real benefits that improve people's lives. It helps people with disabilities communicate more effectively and gives non-native English speakers tools to participate fully in school and work. It provides free tutoring to students who can't afford human tutors. Some argue that AI will free us from boring tasks so we can focus on creative work. Every major technology has faced similar fears, yet humanity has always adapted. The benefits are just as real as the dangers.

ChatGPT isn't going away—the question is whether it will make us smarter or simply dependent. Every time you tell ChatGPT to write something, you're making a choice about your future. We still have time to change course, but we must act now. Write your own essays, solve your own problems, and craft your own messages. ChatGPT is writing our future right now—will we let it write us out of the story, or will we pick up the pen ourselves.
