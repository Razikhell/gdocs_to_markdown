**AI’s Impact on Music**

Every Monday morning, over 100 million Spotify users wake up to a gift: 30 songs picked just for them by an AI called Discover Weekly. Since Spotify launched this feature in 2015, it has become one of the most popular ways people find new music. But have you ever stopped to think about how this little playlist might be changing the entire music industry? I would argue that Spotify's Discover Weekly algorithm is quietly revolutionizing how we listen to music. As listeners, we need to understand these changes and take steps to keep music interesting and diverse.

The old way of becoming a famous musician was tough. You had to get a record deal, hope radio stations played your songs, and pray that people noticed you. Spotify’s Discover Weekly AI is changing that. The AI doesn't care if you're a superstar or someone recording songs in your bedroom. It only cares about whether listeners ***might*** enjoy your music. Discover Weekly has helped unknown artists reach millions of listeners overnight. In 2020, Spotify reported that Discover Weekly had generated over 2.3 billion hours of listening to new artists that users had never heard before. 

That’s not all Discovery Weekly is doing. For almost 100 years, radio was the main way people discovered new music. You'd turn on your favorite station, and a DJ would play songs they thought “you” would like. However, they didn’t know anything about you\! That DJ was playing the same songs for thousands of people at once, hoping that their listeners enjoyed what they heard. Discover Weekly does something radio never could – it creates a unique playlist for each individual listener. Instead of one DJ guessing what a whole city might enjoy, the Discover Weekly AI analyzes your specific listening history and finds songs that match your taste. And it seems to be working. According to Edison Research, the percentage of Americans who listen to traditional AM/FM radio has dropped every year since streaming became popular. Some experts predict that radio as we know it might not exist in 20 years.

Here's where things get complicated. Discover Weekly may  be making everyone's taste in music more similar. Think about it. The AI recommends songs based on what millions of other users with similar tastes have enjoyed. Over time, this creates a feedback loop. Songs that fit the algorithm's patterns get recommended more, which means they get more plays, which means they get recommended even more. Meanwhile, new music that doesn't fit these common patterns gets ignored. This is what experts call a "filter bubble". To make matters worse, music producers have noticed this. Some artists have admitted to making songs specifically designed to "beat the algorithm".  If everyone is trying to make music that pleases the same algorithm, music will begin to sound the same.  
It's important to acknowledge that Discover Weekly isn't all-powerful. People still discover music through friends, social media, movies, and yes, even radio. Spotify has also added features like "Discovery Mode" and genre-specific playlists that try to introduce more variety. Additionally, some would argue that having similar music taste across the world could actually bring people together. Maybe sharing the same songs could help us understand each other better. Plus, if you really want to find weird, new music, you can always go out and try to find it without the help of AI.

Spotify's Discover Weekly is more than just a convenient playlist. It's a powerful force that's reshaping which artists succeed, threatening century-old radio stations, and potentially making global music taste more uniform. Like any powerful tool, it can be used for good or for harm. So what can we do? We can't stop algorithms from existing, but we can make sure we don't let them control everything we hear. I think that Spotify should give you a random user's Discover Weekly playlist every month. Imagine hearing what someone your age in another country loves to listen to\! It would burst our filter bubbles and keep music interesting and diverse.

Until Spotify adds that feature, we can do it ourselves.
