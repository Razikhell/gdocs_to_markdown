**The Basics of Writing an Op-Ed**

**What is an Op-Ed?**

An op-ed is a short article where you share your opinion about something happening in the world and try to convince readers to agree with you. The name "op-ed" comes from "opposite the editorial page" in newspapers—that's where these articles used to be printed\!

---

**Tips Before You Start Writing**

1. **Pick Something Related to a Passion**  
   * Think about what topics you know a lot about. Maybe you're really into video games, climate change, your local community, or a sport you play. That knowledge makes you someone worth listening to\! There’s also a good chance that AI is impacting the topics you know a lot about.

2. **Use Simple Words**  
   * Don't try to sound smart by using big, confusing words. The best writers explain things so clearly that anyone can understand.

3. **Respect Your Reader**  
   * Your readers are smart—they just might not know as much about your topic as you do. It's your job to explain things clearly and make them care.

---

**Questions to Ask Yourself While Writing**

* **Why Should People Believe Me?**  
  * What makes you someone who knows about this topic? Maybe you've experienced something firsthand, done a lot of research, or have a unique perspective.

* **Can I Prove What I'm Saying?**  
  * Do you have facts, examples, or evidence to back up your opinion? Even people who disagree with you should be able to admit your facts are true.

* **Why Should Anyone Care?**  
  * Imagine someone saying "So what?" after reading your article. How would you convince them this matters?

* **Am I Being Fair?**  
  * Are you trying to understand people who disagree with you, or are you just being mean to them? You're more likely to change someone's mind if you treat them with respect.

* **Am I Being Helpful?**  
  * Think about your article as a way to inform your readers of something. What will they learn or gain from reading it?

---

**How to Organize Your Op-Ed**

Here's one way to structure your article:

1. **The Hook (Introduction)**  
   * Start with something that grabs attention and connects to something happening right now. This is called a "news hook" because it ties your article to current news.

2. **Your Main Argument**  
   * Tell readers what you believe and why. This is the big point you're trying to make.

3. **Your Evidence**  
   * Point 1 \- include a fact, example, or story  
   * Add a mini-conclusion for point 1  
   * Point 2 \- include a fact, example, or story  
   * Add a mini-conclusion for point 2  
   * Point 3 \- include a fact, example, or story  
   * Add a mini-conclusion for point 3  
     1. *Tip: Your third point can zoom out and look at the bigger picture. For example, how does this connect to history or why does a problem keep happening?*

4. **The "To Be Sure" Paragraph**  
   * This is where you admit that the other side has some good points. It shows you're being fair and makes your argument stronger because you're not ignoring problems with your own thinking.

5. **Conclusion**  
   * Wrap it up\! Many writers circle back to something from their introduction to make the ending feel complete.  
   * This is also where you should include a “Call to Action” if you want to inspire your readers to do something about the topic.

---

**Remember\!**

These are just suggestions to help you, not strict rules. The most important thing is to have something meaningful to say and to say it clearly. Your voice and ideas matter\!
