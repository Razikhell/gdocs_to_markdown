# The AI That Will Make "Seeing Is Believing" Obsolete

\*\*The Hook\*\*

Picture this: You're scrolling through social media and see a video of your favorite celebrity endorsing a product they've never touched, or a news clip showing a protest that never happened, or even footage of yourself at a place you've never been. Sound like science fiction? It's not anymore. OpenAI just released Sora, an artificial intelligence tool that can create stunningly realistic videos from nothing more than a text description. Type "a cat teaching a classroom of puppies" and within minutes, Sora generates a video so convincing you'd swear someone actually filmed it. As Sora becomes available to millions of users in 2024, we're entering an era where seeing is no longer believing—and we're dangerously unprepared for what comes next.

\*\*My Main Argument\*\*

Sora represents both an incredible creative breakthrough and a serious threat to truth in our society. While this technology will unlock amazing opportunities for storytelling and education, we must require clear labeling of all AI-generated videos before Sora destroys our ability to trust video evidence, eliminates thousands of creative jobs, and becomes a powerful tool for scammers and manipulators. The time to act is now, before fake videos become impossible to distinguish from real ones.

\*\*Point 1: Trust in Video Evidence Will Crumble\*\*

To understand how Sora works, imagine you have a friend who's watched every movie, TV show, and YouTube video ever made. This friend has studied how things move, how shadows fall, how water splashes, and how people walk. Now imagine asking this friend to create a moving film of anything you describe. That's essentially what Sora does—it's been trained on millions of hours of video and learned the patterns of how our world looks and moves. When you type a description, Sora uses this knowledge to generate brand-new video footage that follows all the rules of reality, even though nothing you're seeing actually happened.

The problem is that humans have relied on video evidence for over a century. Courts use security footage to convict criminals. News organizations use video to report events. We share videos to prove things happened. But Sora breaks this system.

Consider what researchers at MIT discovered in 2023: people believe false information 60% more often when it's presented as video rather than text. We're hardwired to trust our eyes. When we see something moving on screen, our brains treat it as real. This made sense when creating fake videos required Hollywood-level resources and expertise. But Sora changes everything—now anyone with a laptop can create convincing fake footage in minutes.

We've already seen the damage that crude deepfakes can cause. In 2023, a fake video of a high school principal making racist comments went viral, destroying his reputation before it was proven false. He lost his job, received death threats, and his family was harassed—all because of a video that wasn't real. That deepfake was relatively easy for experts to identify as fake. Sora's videos are far more sophisticated, making detection much harder.

\*\*Mini-conclusion:\*\* When video evidence becomes unreliable, we lose one of our most important tools for establishing truth, turning every claim into a "he said, she said" argument where nobody can prove what really happened.

\*\*Point 2: Creative Workers Will Face Massive Job Losses\*\*

My neighbor runs a small video production company. He employs five people who shoot commercials for local businesses, create training videos for companies, and film weddings on weekends. He's terrified of Sora, and he should be. Why would a business pay his team $5,000 and wait two weeks for a commercial when they could type a description into Sora and get a professional-looking video in an hour for a fraction of the cost?

This isn't just speculation—we've seen this exact pattern with AI image generators. When tools like DALL-E and Midjourney launched in 2022, they could create professional-quality images from text descriptions. Within 18 months, freelance illustrators reported income drops averaging 37%, according to surveys by the Freelancers Union. Job postings for illustrators on platforms like Upwork fell by 42%. The work didn't disappear—it just stopped going to human artists.

Video production is a much bigger industry than illustration. According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, over 300,000 Americans work directly in video production, from camera operators to editors to animators. Millions more work in related fields like acting, set design, and lighting. These are solid middle-class jobs that support families and communities. A 2024 report by McKinsey & Company estimates that AI video generation tools could automate up to 45% of tasks currently performed by video production workers within the next five years, potentially displacing over 135,000 jobs in the U.S. alone. Sora threatens to accelerate this displacement dramatically.

Some people argue that AI will create new jobs to replace the old ones, but history suggests otherwise. When factories automated, we didn't get an equal number of robot maintenance jobs—we got a handful of technician positions and millions of unemployed factory workers. The same pattern will likely repeat with Sora: a few AI specialists will get rich while thousands of video professionals lose their livelihoods.

\*\*Mini-conclusion:\*\* Sora will accelerate job losses in creative industries, concentrating wealth among tech companies while devastating workers who spent years building skills that are suddenly obsolete.

\*\*Point 3: Scammers and Manipulators Gain a Powerful New Weapon\*\*

Let's zoom out and look at the bigger picture: what happens when bad actors get their hands on Sora? The possibilities are genuinely frightening.

Scammers are already using AI voice cloning to impersonate people's relatives and trick them into sending money. The FBI reported that these scams stole over $11 million in 2023 alone. Now imagine adding video to these scams. A panicked video call from your "daughter" saying she's been arrested and needs bail money immediately becomes far more convincing than just a voice call. Elderly people, who are already the primary targets of scams, will be especially vulnerable.

Political manipulation will reach new levels. We've already seen how edited videos and misleading clips can influence elections. In 2020, a selectively edited video of Nancy Pelosi appearing drunk went viral and was viewed millions of times before being debunked. That was just clever editing of real footage. With Sora, political operatives can create entirely fabricated videos of candidates saying outrageous things, accepting bribes, or meeting with controversial figures. Even when these videos are eventually proven false, the damage to a candidate's reputation may be permanent. Studies show that corrections rarely reach as many people as the original false information.

Perhaps most disturbing is the potential for harassment and abuse. Revenge porn—sharing intimate images without consent—already ruins lives. Now imagine ex-partners creating fake videos of you in compromising situations. Bullies creating humiliating videos of classmates. Stalkers fabricating evidence to blackmail victims. The Cyber Civil Rights Initiative found that 90% of revenge porn victims experience significant psychological trauma, and many lose jobs or face damaged relationships. Sora makes this kind of abuse easier and more convincing than ever before.

\*\*Mini-conclusion:\*\* Without proper safeguards, Sora becomes a weapon that amplifies scams, political manipulation, and personal harassment, with the most vulnerable people suffering the worst consequences.

\*\*To Be Sure\*\*

I want to be fair here: Sora isn't purely evil, and there are legitimate concerns about over-regulating new technology.

Sora offers real benefits. Independent filmmakers who could never afford professional equipment can now create their visions. Teachers can generate historical recreations to make lessons engaging. Scientists can visualize complex concepts. Small nonprofits can create compelling videos to promote their causes without expensive production costs. These are genuine positives that could democratize video creation and give voice to people who were previously locked out.

There's also a valid argument that we're being too alarmist. When photography was invented, people worried it would replace painting. When Photoshop launched, people feared photo manipulation would destroy trust in images. Yet society adapted to both technologies. We learned to be more skeptical, developed verification methods, and life went on. Maybe we'll adapt to AI video generation too.

Additionally, heavy-handed regulation could stifle innovation and push development to countries with fewer rules, making the technology harder to control rather than easier.

These are fair points. We shouldn't let fear of new technology prevent us from exploring its benefits, and we should be thoughtful about how we regulate rather than rushing into poorly designed rules.

However, there's a crucial difference between Sora and previous technologies: the speed and scale of its impact. Photoshop required significant skill to use convincingly; Sora requires only the ability to type. Photography captured reality; Sora fabricates it entirely. We had decades to adapt to previous media technologies; Sora is being released into a world already drowning in misinformation, where a fake video can reach billions of people in hours. The risks are simply too great to take a "wait and see" approach.

\*\*Conclusion and Call to Action\*\*

Remember that video of the cat teaching puppies I mentioned at the start? It sounds charming and harmless. But the same technology that creates that cute video can create footage of you committing a crime you didn't commit, a politician accepting a bribe that never happened, or your child in a dangerous situation that's completely fabricated. The technology doesn't distinguish between harmless fun and serious harm—it just creates whatever someone asks for.

We're at a crossroads. We can either act now to put safeguards in place, or we can wait until the damage is done and try to clean up the mess afterward. History suggests the second option doesn't work well.

My recommendation is straightforward: require mandatory, visible watermarks on all AI-generated videos. Every video created by Sora or similar tools should have a clear, permanent label identifying it as AI-generated. This label should be embedded in the video file itself, making it difficult to remove, and should be visible when the video plays. Think of it like the "PAID ADVERTISEMENT" labels required on sponsored content, or the warning labels on cigarette packages—simple, clear disclosure that helps people make informed decisions about what they're watching.

This isn't about banning technology or preventing innovation. It's about transparency. People have a right to know whether they're watching something real or something generated by AI. This simple requirement would prevent most of the serious harms I've described while still allowing the beneficial uses of the technology to flourish.

Some will argue that watermarks can be removed or that bad actors will ignore the rules. That's true, but it's not a reason to do nothing. We don't abandon speed limits just because some people speed. We don't eliminate food safety laws just because some companies violate them. Rules work by making harmful behavior harder and creating consequences for those who break them. Watermarking won't stop every abuse, but it will stop most of them, and it will make it easier to identify and prosecute those who create harmful fake videos.

\*\*Here's how you can help make this happen:\*\*

First, contact your elected representatives. It takes just a few minutes to send an email or make a phone call to your congressional representative, senator, or local officials. Tell them you're concerned about AI-generated videos and support requiring clear labeling. Politicians pay attention when constituents speak up—but only if we actually do it. You can find contact information for your representatives at house.gov and senate.gov.

As your constituent, I'm writing to express concern about AI-generated video tools like Sora. I urge you to support legislation requiring mandatory watermarks on all AI-generated videos to protect against misinformation and fraud. This technology poses serious risks to truth and trust in our society, and we need transparency requirements before it becomes widespread. Thank you for considering this important issue.

Second, support organizations working on this issue. Groups like the Partnership on AI, the AI Now Institute, and the Center for Democracy and Technology are researching AI harms and advocating for responsible policies. They need public support and funding to counter the lobbying power of big tech companies. Follow them on social media, share their work, and donate if you can.

Third, spread awareness. Talk to your friends and family about AI-generated videos. Share articles like this one. The more people understand what's possible with tools like Sora, the harder it becomes for tech companies to release these tools without proper safeguards. Public pressure works—we've seen it force companies to change policies on privacy, content moderation, and other issues.

Fourth, practice good digital literacy yourself. Start questioning videos you see online, especially if they show something shocking or outrageous. Check if reputable news sources are reporting the same thing. Look for signs of manipulation. Teach these skills to others, especially young people and elderly relatives who may be most vulnerable to deception.

The window for action is closing fast. Once millions of people have access to Sora and similar tools, once fake videos flood the internet, once trust in video evidence collapses completely—it will be much harder to put safeguards in place. We have a brief opportunity right now, while the technology is still new, to establish rules that protect society while allowing innovation to continue.

That cat teaching puppies is adorable. But the same technology that creates that innocent video can create a nightmare scenario where we can't trust anything we see. The choice is ours: act now to require transparency, or live in a world where seeing is no longer believing.

What will you choose?
