**Instagram’s Ai Models and Algorithm: How it’s Affecting You**

Last week, my 12-year-old cousin showed me her Instagram feed. In just five minutes of scrolling, she saw seventeen videos about extreme dieting, four posts romanticizing anxiety, and countless ads for products she'd casually mentioned wanting. "The algorithm just \*knows\* me," she said with a nervous laugh. But here's what worried me: the algorithm doesn't just know her—it's actively deciding who she becomes.

Instagram's AI models have become one of the most powerful forces shaping how young people see themselves and the world. It's time we recognize these algorithms for what they really are: invisible puppet masters that need serious oversight.

As someone who's spent months studying how social media affects teenagers—and as a former Instagram power-user myself—I've watched these AI systems evolve from simple chronological feeds to sophisticated prediction machines. Instagram now uses multiple AI models working simultaneously: one for your main feed, another for Stories, a third for Explore, and yet another for Reels. Each one is constantly learning from every tap, pause, and scroll you make.

Here's what makes this so concerning: these algorithms don't just respond to your interests—they \*amplify\* them. Research from the Wall Street Journal's 2021 investigation showed that Instagram's algorithm can push users down rabbit holes in a matter of days. When researchers created accounts interested in extreme dieting, the algorithm quickly flooded those feeds with content about eating disorders. The AI models prioritize "engagement" above all else, and unfortunately, extreme content keeps people scrolling.

The numbers back this up. According to Instagram's own internal research leaked by whistleblower Frances Haugen, 32% of teen girls said that when they felt bad about their bodies, Instagram made them feel worse. The algorithm wasn't just reflecting their insecurities—it was feeding them.

Instagram's AI doesn't show you reality—it shows you a customized version of reality designed to keep you on the app. The algorithm tracks hundreds of signals: who you interact with most, what you save, how long you watch videos, even whether you turn on sound for certain types of content. It uses something called "collaborative filtering," which means if you like the same posts as someone else, Instagram will start showing you what \*they\* like too.

This creates what I call "algorithmic peer pressure." You're not just influenced by your actual friends anymore—you're influenced by thousands of anonymous users the AI has decided are similar to you. If those users engage with content about luxury lifestyles, conspiracy theories, or unhealthy beauty standards, guess what starts appearing in your feed?

A 2022 study from the University of Amsterdam found that Instagram's algorithm can shift users' interests in as little as one week of use. Participants who showed mild interest in fitness content were soon being served extreme workout videos and supplement ads. The algorithm doesn't care about balance—it cares about engagement, and extreme content is engaging.

This matters beyond individual users. When millions of people are each trapped in their own algorithmic bubble, we lose our shared reality. We stop seeing what's actually happening in the world and start seeing only what the AI thinks will keep us scrolling.

This isn't just about Instagram—it's about who gets to shape an entire generation's worldview. Throughout history, we've regulated powerful institutions that influence young people. We have standards for what can be shown in schools, what advertisers can say to children, and what content can appear on broadcast television. But social media algorithms operate in a regulatory vacuum.

Instagram's parent company, Meta, makes over $100 billion annually from advertising. Their business model depends on keeping you on the app as long as possible, and their AI models are optimized for exactly that goal—not for your wellbeing, not for truth, and not for healthy development. We've essentially handed one of the most influential forces in young people's lives to a company whose primary obligation is to its shareholders.

The pattern keeps repeating because we keep treating social media like it's neutral technology rather than what it actually is: a massively powerful media company that should be held to similar standards as other media.

I'll admit that Instagram's algorithm isn't all bad. It helps small creators find audiences, connects people with niche interests, and can surface genuinely helpful content. When my friend was struggling with coming out, Instagram's algorithm connected her with LGBTQ+ support communities that made a real difference in her life. The AI can also filter out some spam and harmful content that would otherwise flood our feeds.

Instagram has also made some efforts to address concerns. They've added features to limit political content, created "sensitivity screens" for potentially upsetting posts, and claim to down-rank content that might be harmful. These are steps in the right direction, even if they don't go far enough.  
We can't put the algorithmic genie back in the bottle, but we can demand transparency and accountability. Here's what needs to happen:

Require social media companies to disclose how their algorithms work and allow independent researchers to study their effects. The EU's Digital Services Act is a good start—the U.S. needs similar legislation.  
Give users real control. Let us choose chronological feeds, see why we're being shown specific content, and opt out of algorithmic recommendations entirely without losing access to the platform.  
Become algorithm-aware. Regularly clear your search history, diversify who you follow, and actively seek out perspectives different from what your feed shows you. Treat your engagement like votes—because that's exactly what they are.

My cousin is still on Instagram, and I'm not suggesting she delete it. But she deserves to know that the feed she's scrolling isn't a neutral window into the world—it's a carefully constructed reality designed by AI models optimized for engagement, not for her wellbeing.

The algorithm doesn't just know you. It's deciding who you'll become. And that's too much power to leave unexamined.
